Radon Levels in Ohio — By County & ZIP Code

Ohio is one of the highest-radon states in the country. The EPA classifies most of Ohio in Zone 1 — the highest radon potential category — where average indoor radon levels are predicted to exceed the EPA action level of 4.0 pCi/L. Radon levels vary significantly by county and ZIP code based on local geology, and the only way to know your home's actual level is to test it.

Understanding Ohio Radon Risk

Ohio's elevated radon levels are primarily the result of geology. The state sits atop uranium-rich shale, limestone, and glacial soils that generate radon gas. As radon migrates upward through the soil, it enters homes through foundation cracks, construction joints, and openings around service pipes. Homes with basements are particularly susceptible, though slab-on-grade homes can also have elevated levels.

The EPA Zone system provides a county-level estimate of radon potential, but it does not predict the radon level in any individual home. Within any Zone 1 county, some homes may test below 2 pCi/L while neighboring homes test above 10 pCi/L. Testing your specific home is the only reliable method.

What Should Ohio Homeowners Do?

The EPA recommends that all homes be tested for radon, regardless of location. If your home's radon level is at or above 4.0 pCi/L, the EPA recommends taking action. Radon mitigation systems can reduce indoor radon levels by 50–99% at a typical installed cost of $1,200–$2,500. Mitigation is cost-effective compared to the long-term health risk.

Ohio-licensed radon testers and mitigation contractors are trained and certified by the Ohio Department of Health. Use our directory to find a professional near you.
